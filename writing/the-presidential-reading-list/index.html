The Presidential Reading List

I’ve been fascinated by the Presidency of the United States for many years. I can probably trace it back to my childhood, as I grew up in an area that’s rich in American history. Just a mile or so from my house, On Christmas Eve 1775, George Washington crossed the Delaware River and engaged the Hessian soldiers in the Battle of Trenton. The battle was a turning point for the war and the man. Morale had been low and the weather severe. The Continental Army had suffered many defeats at the hands of the British and the revolution was starting to crumble. But Washington was stoic. He devised a brash plan and under the cover of darkness, crossed the icy Delaware for a suprise attack. That small victory singlehandedly turned the tide of the Revolution, and the myth of George Washington began.

Today we are intimately familiar with his myth, from the cherry tree to his wooden teeth. But what about the real man behind the anecdotes? Who was he? How did he become the Father of our Nation? The challenge faced by any biographer of Washington is wading through the mythology to find the truth lurking beneath.

Washington doesn’t stand alone.

Trenton is fascinating, but it has nothing on Philadelphia. The original Capitol of the United States is brimming with presidential history, and is the place where the idea of the Presidency came to fruition.

These places are fascinating in their own right, but what about the men themselves? I knew very little about most, and almost nothing about others. This is pretty common. What can you tell me about John Tyler1? Benjamin Harrison? Outside of a useless 5th grade assignment where I had to memorize their order (but nothing about them), I didn’t start to learn about their lives until later in life. Even so, my research has been haphazard. So I’m starting a new reading project. I’ll be reading biographies of the U.S. Presidents, in order, starting with Washington: A Life by Ron Chernow.

I don’t intend for this to be a sprint but a lifelong project. I want to think deeply about the people who have guided our country through difficult times, and the circumstances which put them in that position. I hope to learn something about the nature of power, politics, and history – and when I do, I’ll post it here. I do solemnly swear.

  1. Here’s a fact about John Tyler that will blow your mind. He was born in 1790, and he has two living grandchildren. ↩
