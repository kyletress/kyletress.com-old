The Wright Brothers

by David McCullough

I think David McCullough is one of the finest popular history writers of our time. With The Wright Brothers he has delivered a rich account of the birth of powered flight. But this isn’t a book about airplanes – it’s a biography of the two bicycle mechanics who willed them into existence through sheer intellect and unwavering commitment.

McCullough drew from the Wright’s meticulous diaries and personal correspondence. The result is an intimate look into the lives of Wilbur and Orville as they work in secret on solving the problem of powered flight.

Some takeaways from this book

  1. This era was ripe with innovation. From photography to the automobile to the airplane, enterprising people were envisioning the future and actively building it, despite ridicule and mockery. Comparisons can be made to our present era of innovation.

  2. The Wright Brothers have a lot to teach us about courage, intellect, and dedication. They were risking their lives to solve the problem of powered flight, and they weren’t doing it for money or fame. They didn’t have a college education. They weren’t scientists or engineers. They were bicycle mechanics from Ohio.

  3. Every time McCullough wrote about Wilbur or Orville taking to the sky1, I’d white-knuckle my chair. It’s easy to forget how dangerous the entire undertaking was.

  1. They rarely flew together. In the event of a fatal crash, one would live to carry on their work. ↩
